## 1\. Waiting Until Launch to Start Marketing

One of the most dangerous traps we see developers fall into is treating "Marketing" as something that happens _after_ the "Build" phase is over. If you wait until your game is live on Steam to start telling people about it, you’re already behind.

In 2026, the digital landscape is noisier than ever. Steam’s visibility algorithms often require a solid base of wishlists: sometimes over 1,000: just to give your game a baseline of organic traffic on launch day. If you haven't built that momentum early, your launch might feel more like a quiet whisper than a **special** event.

**Instead of waiting, do this:**

*   **Start a Devlog:** Share your progress from day one. People love seeing the "behind the scenes" of game development.
*   **Share Early Prototypes:** Don't be afraid to show off unpolished gameplay. It builds authenticity and lets your future players feel like they're part of the journey. 🧑‍💻
*   **Collect Wishlists Early:** Use every post and every clip to drive people to your store page or your [Gamefolio profile](https://app.gamefolio.com).

## 2\. Neglecting Visual-First Platforms

We’re living in a visual-first world. A block of text explaining your game’s complex lore is great, but a 10-second clip of a **charming** animation or a high-octane combat sequence is what actually stops the scroll.

Many developers rely too heavily on static screenshots or long-winded descriptions. If you aren't showcasing your game through high-quality video clips and reels, you’re missing out on the primary way modern gamers discover new content.

![Visual Gaming Clips](/assets/7-mistakes-youre-making-with-indie-game-marketing-and-how-to-fix-them/inline-1.webp)

**How to fix it:**

*   **Record Everything:** Every time you implement a cool new visual effect or a funny bug occurs, record it!
*   **Focus on the "Hook":** Your clips should show off your game's unique selling point within the first 3 seconds.
*   **Leverage Reels:** Platforms like [Gamefolio's Latest Reels](https://app.gamefolio.com/latest-reels) are designed specifically to promote these short, punchy moments to an audience that's already looking for their next favorite game.

## 3\. Ignoring Community Engagement

Marketing isn't a monologue; it's a conversation. A huge mistake is treating your social media like a billboard: just posting updates and never looking at the comments. Your early adopters are your most **essential** marketing asset. They are the ones who will tell their friends, leave reviews, and keep the buzz alive.

If you aren't engaging with your community, they’ll eventually stop engaging with you. You need to foster a space where players feel heard and valued.

**Instead of shouting, do this:**

*   **Join the Conversation:** Reply to comments, ask for feedback, and run polls about game features.
*   **Build a Hub:** Whether it’s an active Discord server or a dedicated community page, give your fans a place to hang out.
*   **Connect on Gamefolio:** We’ve built an active [Discord community](https://gamefolio.com/about) specifically so developers and gamers can connect in real-time. Use these spaces to build genuine relationships, not just to post links. 💬

## 4\. Using Only "Generic" Social Media

Twitter, Instagram, and TikTok are great, but they aren't built _just_ for gamers. Your content is competing with everything from cat videos to political debates. When you rely solely on generic platforms, your reach is often throttled by algorithms that don't understand the nuances of the gaming community.

To truly grow, you need to be where the gamers are. You need a platform that understands what a "clip" is, why an "achievement" matters, and how to get your content in front of people who are specifically looking for indie games.

## 5\. Forgetting App Store & Platform Optimization (ASO)

It doesn’t matter how good your marketing is if people can’t find your game when they search for it. Many indie devs treat their game’s title, keywords, and descriptions as an afterthought. This is a massive missed opportunity for organic discovery.

Whether it’s on the Steam store, the Epic Games Store, or a community platform, you need to speak the language of search engines.

**How to fix it:**

*   **Keyword Research:** What are people searching for? "Roguelike deckbuilder"? "Charming cozy game"? Use these terms in your descriptions.
*   **A/B Test Your Visuals:** Try different thumbnails and banners to see what gets the highest click-through rate.
*   **Keep It Fresh:** Regularly update your profile and listings with new clips and news to stay relevant in the "Recent Updates" sections.

## 6\. Weak Press and Influencer Outreach

Blast-emailing 500 journalists with a generic template is not a marketing strategy: it's a recipe for the spam folder. Journalists and streamers receive hundreds of pitches a day. If your pitch doesn't immediately show them why your game is **special**, it will be ignored.

The biggest mistake here is not doing your homework. You shouldn't be pitching a horror game to a streamer who only plays cozy farming sims.

**Instead of "Spray and Pray," do this:**

*   **Personalize Your Pitches:** Mention a specific video or article they’ve done and explain why their audience would love your game.
*   **Provide a Press Kit:** Make it incredibly easy for them to cover you. Include high-res logos, screenshots, and a punchy trailer.
*   **Build Momentum First:** Influencers are more likely to cover a game that already has some "social proof." Showcase your community growth on [Gamefolio](https://app.gamefolio.com/explore) to prove there's interest!

## 7\. Treating Launch Day as the "End"

The finish line isn't the day you hit "Publish." In fact, for many successful indie games, launch day is just the beginning of the real work. If you stop marketing the moment your game is out, your sales will likely plummet after the initial release spike.

Post-launch marketing is about retention and reaching the "late majority" of players who wait for reviews and updates before buying.

![Growth and Optimization](/assets/7-mistakes-youre-making-with-indie-game-marketing-and-how-to-fix-them/inline-3.webp)

**How to fix it:**

*   **Plan Post-Launch Content:** Roadmap your updates, DLCs, or even just small quality-of-life patches and market them as "New Experiences."
*   **Share User-Generated Content:** Highlight clips and screenshots created by your players. This not only rewards your community but shows potential buyers that people are active and having fun.
*   **Stay Active:** Continue to share your game’s journey. Every milestone: 1,000 players, first anniversary, major update: is a new marketing opportunity. 📈
